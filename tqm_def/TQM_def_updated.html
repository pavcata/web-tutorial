Total Quality Management

What is Total Quality Management (TQM)?

Total quality management (TQM) is a strategy aiming to improve quality, efficiency, and productivity. A key goal of TQM is to enable companies to provide a high-quality customer service. It transforms the firm into a highly competitive organization with strategic focus and definite plans for the long run. TQM is about continuous improvement of production processes, manifested by quality measurement and defect reduction techniques, customer focus, cost and waste reduction, statistical process control, and changes in organizational philosophy. Company personnel at all levels of the company take part in TQM. In this respect training, culture, teamwork, and common strategic goals are all an essential part of the TQM implementation.

Common TQM practices:

  • "Statistical process control is the use of statistical process control charts to provide operators with feedback, allowing them to base their actions on the variability of the manufacturing process.
  • Product design for quality includes improving design characteristics through design for manufacturability and reliability engineering.
  • Throughout customer focus is designed to better assess customer needs and an organization's effectiveness in meeting them.
  • Information feedback provides the workforce with process and performance information to assist with decentralized management of the production process and variance minimization.
  • Management support includes communication of strategy, development of corporate culture, or the social energy that drives the organization" (1).

Relationship between just in time (JIT) and TQM

"Worldclass manufacturing is a synthesis of just-in-time (JIT), total quality management (TQM), employee involvement, preventive maintenance practices, computer-integrated manufacturing, and strategic management. Hence, our consultancy offers implementation of JIT in parallel with TQM. The two approaches have common practices and evidence suggests that JIT practices improve quality performance through problem exposure and improved process feedback. "JIT practices affect quality performance by reducing lot sizes, which reduces potential scrap and rework resulting from process failure. Furthermore, inventory reduction practices associated with JIT induce immediate parts starvation in the event of a process problem, forcing fast and concerted problem-solving efforts, with obvious benefits for quality performance. These and other relationships are postulated to cause JIT and TQM approaches to function more effectively when used in unison"(1).
